Creating a Morph for a NPC in Dragon Age Origins

When it comes to modding a game, it can seem quite daunting, even when looking to mod something small. For Dragon Age: Origins, modding has been made easier by the release of the Dragon Age Toolset. Though with the loss of BioWare forums and with Dragon Age: Origins being 12 years old, finding instructions on how to use the Dragon Age Toolset can be hard to find.

One of the easy ways to mod the game with the toolset is by creating a Morph. A Morph is more or less, the head of a character, the model of their face. One of the easiest things to do with a morph, is create one for an NPC. The process only requires the Dragon Age Toolset, and can be completed in a matter of minutes.

These instructions will go through step by step how to create a morph and then replace a Non Player Character's default morph.

Required:

  • Dragon Age: Origins installed on your PC
  • The Dragon Age Toolset installed

Recommended:

  • An existing save with your character near the NPC who's morph you are overriding

This section will cover the creation of a Morph in the Dragon Age Toolset.

The morph itself is the 3d model of the character's head. This model can be edited much like the way one can create a player character in the game itself, with customizing sliders and selections. However, nagivating the toolset and editing a morph in the toolset functions differently from the in-game character creator.

When opening the program, first you need to create a new file. In the menu bar under file > new, and select the Morph option in the dropdown menu.

Opening the File

In the Dragon Age Toolset, click on the File in the top left-hand corner, in the dropdown menu, then hover over New. In the dropdown menu that appears, locate Morph which is located roughly halfway down the list. Click Morph, this will create a new morph file.

Nothing will appear at first, because in order to edit the morph, you need to select the base morph. Dragon Age: Origins has seperate morphs for each race and gender. Humans, for example, are split into the HM (Human Male) morph and the HF (Human Female) morph.

Selecting Base

The window will remain blank until you select an option in the [Choose Base] dropdown list. This is located in the main toolbar under the Help menu.

In the dropdown box, you need to select the base for the character you are creating a morph for. The first letter is the race (Human, Elf, Dwarf) and second letter is the gender (Male, Female).

Selecting Base Cont.

In the dropdown menu, the base models are listed. You need to select the appropriate base for the character you are creating a morph for. The models are: HM (Human Male), HF (Human Female), DM (Dwarf Male), DF (Dwarf Female), EM (Elf Male), EF (Elf Female), QM (Qunari Male), and KN (Human child).

For example, if you are creating a morph for Leliana, her morph needs to be HF.

Selecting Base Cont.

The morph needed for a character is simple to discern, as it is simply their race and gender. For the example, we will create a morph for Leliana. Leliana is a human female, so she needs the HF morph. Once a base is selected, the base will appear. Unlike the game, the toolset does not have preset faces aside from the base, so all morphs must be created from the appropriate base.

Rotating Model

The model can be rotated by holding shift then holding right click with the mouse and dragging it.

Rotating the Model

Instead of a button for rotation, rotating the model requires holding the SHIFT key while holding the right click button on the mouse and then moving the mouse. This rotates the model on the horizontal axis (left and right).

Moving Camera

By holding CTRL and right clicking and dragging, you can rotate the camera on the positive vertical axis (you can roate above the morph, but not below) and on the horizontal axis.

Rotating the Camera

The camera in the toolset can be rotated more than the model itself. By holding Control (CTRL) and holding the right mouse button while draggon the mouse, the camera can be rotated up and side to side. The camera cannot rotate under the model. At the default distance, when rotating up the camera clips (passes through) the model.

By scrolling with the mouse wheel, you can move the camera in or out, you can also freely move the camera by holding CTRL and dragging left click.

Moving the Camera

To move the camera's position, you hold Control (CTRL) and hold left click while dragging the mouse. The Toolset's controls are sensitive, so small mouse movements corresponds to large camera movements.

At any point, you can right click in the display and click the option zoom to object that appears. This will return the camera to its default zoom, but will keep the angle the camera has.

Resetting the Camera

At any point, you can left click with the mouse on the display window. A small menu will pop up, and clicking the option Zoom to Object in the menu will effectively reset the camera's position. Any changes made to the camera's position or zoom will be undone and the camera will revert to its default placement.

View Settings

Under the object inspector on the right hand side of the application, one of the tabs available is the View Settings tab. Under this tab, positioning of the model and camera can be edited directly with numerical values.

Changing View Settings

Instead of using the mouse, the camera and model's position and rotation can be changed in the View Settings tab in the Object Inspector. The 13th tab in the object inspector has the numerical data for the camera and model position and rotation. These fields can be edited directly. This can be helpful especially for precise movements or angles you may desire.

This section will walk through the technical side of editing the morph, such as methods of editing and how unique aspects are edited. These are not instructions on the artistic aspect of creating a morph, as there is no one correct way to create a morph, only a correct way to use the tools.

General Controls

To customize the morph, you simply edit the values shown in the Object Inspector window. Within the Object Inspector there are 4 types of formats for customization options, these are file selection (kebab menu), value slider, mutually exclusive sliders, and multi option Sliders.

File Selection

File selection is used in fields that have one thing that needs to be selected, such as tint files (the color of something, such as skin, hair, or makeup), textures, tattoos, beards, or hair. These may appear alongside sliders.

In some fields, the value field shows a kebab button (a button with 3 dots, ...). Selecting that will prompt a pop-up window in which a file must be chosen. All the files are from the game files, and the correct directory is already open, all that is required is to select a file from the window.

Selecting a File

For some fields, there may be data or a blank area follwed by a kebab button, a button with 3 dots. For these fields, click the kebab button. This will bring up a window that allows the selection of a file for what is being customized. In this example, we are selecting the tint file (color) for the lip makeup.

Sliders

Sliders are the typical way to customize in game, something that carries over to the toolset. Unlike the game, however, the sliders in the toolset are also commonly used for opacity (transparency).

For customization of things like the nose, eyes, mouth, etc. sliders are used identically to the game: the slider corresponds to its titled value. For example, the slider for nose bridge width changes the width of the morph's nose bridge width.

Sometimes, however, the toolset will pair both a file selection and a slider. In these cases, the slider corresponds to the opacity or intensity of the file selected. For things like makeup, tattoos, and eyebrows, the color or shape is selected, and the slider then allows to adjust the opacity of the color.

Using a Slider

Similar to the character creation in game, sliders work by right clicking and dragging the pointer on the slider. The slider value can also be changed by clicking the slider field and scrolling with the mouse wheel. Some sliders for features allow for negative value, for the example though, the slider is for opacity so it cannot go below 0.

Mutually Exclusive Sliders

Some sliders in the toolset present multiple possible sliders, but only one can be chosen.

For some features, there are multiple sliders presented, such as for eye shape or for eyebrows. Only one slider can be moved, by moving one slider to a value above the base value of 0, the other sliders are greyed out and cannot be used.

Using Mutally Exclusive Sliders

For some features, numerous sliders are presented but only one can be used. By changing the value of the slider, the other slider options become inaccesible. This can be undone by setting the slider to 0. In the example, the eyebrows sliders are mutually exclusive, The slider's value corresponds to the opacity of the slider's eyebrow.

Multi Option Sliders

Differing from the mutually-exclusive sliders, multi option sliders allow for the sliders to be used concurrently.

Notably, this is used for nose shapes. There are numerous preset nose shapes which can be used with a slider, but all the sliders can be used at the same time. This allows for creating a nose shape that is a mix of multiple shapes.

Using Multi-Option Sliders

For a few sliders, numerous options are presented, but unlike the mutually exclusive sliders, all the sliders may be toggled and edited. In the example, we are using the slider for multiple nose shapes to create a nose shape that is a mix between the two preset shapes.


Special Sections

In the Object Inspector there are a total of 15 tabs, 13 of which contain options for customization, while some are self-explanitory in their customization and options, some have unique properties. Specifically, the Matierials section and the Part Selection section have special properties.

Materials

The Materials tab covers the customization of the skin tone, make up color, make up opacity (transparency), and skin texture. While the skin tone and make up follows the simple process of selections and sliders, the skin texture customization is slightly more complex.

Under the Material tab, the Skin Texture section has the selection for a base texture, and blend texture. The default blend texture is the texture for an old person. The available selections for the vanilla (unmodded) game are severely limited. The selection can be expanded by downloading mods, if a skin texture is desired, however skin textures and types are not neccesary for a morph.

Skin Texture Options

Within the material tab, find the section labeled "Skin Texture". The blend texture is the texture which will be used for skin texture and type. With the selected texture, you can overlay the texture in certain areas at any opacity.

The sliders below allow for the blend texture to be overlaid over the base texture at varying opacities. The Skin Texture will change the coloring of the skin.

Step X

With the blend sliders under the Skin Texture section, these correspond to the coloring of the texture selected but does not change the constrution of the 3-d model. In this example, the blend option selected is the base game's "old" texture, and the skin texture slider changes the opacity of the colors from the "old" texture.

The Skin Type section below that functions similar to the Skin Texture, but instead of adding color to the base, it will change the 3d texture associated with the blend. For the old texture, this will give the model wrinkles but with no skin discoloration or shading.

Step X

Below the Skin Texture section you will find the Skin Type section. The Skin Type section has the same set up of sliders that the Skin Texture section had, however these sliders correlate to the 3-d aspect of the skin textures. In the example, the "old" texture changes the surface of the morph is to have wrinkles

Part Selection

In Part selection, details such as eye color, hair color, hair, beard (male only), stubble, and eyebrows. It also is where tattoos can be selected, however, single tattoos are not broken up into files for selection, but rather, a single tattoo file holds 4 tattoo designs which are visible via slider options.

Under the first field under the Tattoo section, the option Tattoo Mask is the selection for the tattoo file. While the file names are somewhat informative of the files content (av is Avaar tattoos, dw is Dwarf tattoos, ed is Dalish Elf tattoos) each file contains 4 tattoos so you might need to check a few tattoo files if you are lookng for a specific tattoo.

Selecing a Tattoo

In the Tattoo section, the tattoo file is selected in the "Tattoo Mask" field via the file selection method seen earlier. The files are broken up into groups, mainly Avaar, Elven, and Dwarven. In the example, we are selecting an Avaar tattoo file.

How the tattoo files work is a single tattoo is stored in each color channel of the file. One in the Red, Blue, Green, and Alpha channels. The sliders for the channels as such serve as sliders for a single tattoos opacity.

Selecting a Tattoo cont.

An indidual tattoo is selected with a slider. The file selected in the previous step contains 4 tattoos, all of which can be used similtaniously. The sliders are labeled as channels, this is because the tattoo stores a unique tattoo in each color channel. In the example, you can see the 4 tattoos which are stored in the Avaar tattoo file we selected.

Once your morph has been customized to your liking, the next step is to save it. To properly save and export it, first its important to know what morph files do what, and the proper method of exporting.

At this point, in your toolset, you should notice the file name shown is Untitled.mrh.

The MRH File

At this point, you should have a morph to your liking. You may notice in the application window, this morph is listed as Untitled.mrh. This is the current name of the file, and the current file format.

An MRH File is the editable file of a morph, not a file that the game can use. The file the game needs for a character's morph is a MOR file, which cannot be edited. While the MRH file is not needed after the morph is exported, it is strongly recommeneded that the MRH is saved, as without it, there is no way to edit the morph.

To save the file, simple click file > save as, and then name and save the file.

Saving the MRH File

The MRH file can be saved just as any file, click the File option in the top left corner, then click the Save As option in the menu. A window will appear in which you can choose where the file is being saved, and what the file's name is. In the example, since it is a morph of leliana, we saved it as Leliana.mrh. Saving this file is the only way to edit it in the future.

.MRH is the editable version of the morph file.
.MOR is the exported, uneditable version of the morph.

Before the morph can be exported, it must be saved. It does not matter where the file is saved, as the .mrh file is not one the game uses. As stated, though, it is strongly recommended to keep the file. The .mrh file can be named anything, it is recommended that you name it something descriptive, such as the name of the character you are creating a morph for.

Once the .mrh file has been saved, the file can now be exported. To do this, you can either right click and choose the Post to Local option that appears, or alternatively, Post to Local can be located under Tools > Export > Post to Local.

Exporting the File

Once the file has been saved as a mrh, the file can be exported by right clicking and selecting the option Post to Local. Once clicked, the process is automatic and requires no further input from you.

In the console in the toolset, it will read out whether the export was successful, and importantly, the location of where the file exported. Rememeber the location of the file as it is neccesary for the next step.

Reading the Console

The console located in the bottom left of the application will read the output of the export process, including whether the file exported sucessfully and where the file was exported to. The location of the exported file should be noted as the final steps involve locating the file in your file explorer. In the example, our morph was successfully exported to the ToolSetExport file in our Override folder.

The final step is to give the file the correct name and place it in the correct override folder. Both steps are essential, as the override will not work if the file is not correctly named or if it is in the wrong folder.

The exported file can be found in the directory the console shows. When it is exported, it will be export with the base prefix (as explained in the Creating a Morph section).

Locating the MOR File

In your file explorer, locate the exported .MOR file. The exact location can be found by reading the console as mentioned in the previous step. Regardless of your file's name, the exported MOR file will export with a prefix of the base model's abbrevation. For the example, the file was saved as Leliana.MRH but exported as HF_Leliana.MOR.

In order to override a Non Player Character's morph, first you need to find what that NPC's moprh is named. This can be found using the Dragon Age Toolset. In the Palette Window on the righthand side of the window, find the Creature section which is shown with the fox head icon.

Locate Creature List

On the top right-hand side, the creatures list can be found inside the palette window. The creatures list has an icon of a fox head. Click on this to bring up the creature list.

In the Creature list, you can find the NPC you are overriding by what part of the game they are in. If they are a companion, their file can be found under Global > Followers. Once you locate the character, clicking on the name will bring up the character's information in the Object Inspector.

Finding the NPC

The creature list is organized by the game chapter for minor NPCs, but companions and major recurring characters are located under the Global folder. Once you locate the NPC you are planning to override, click on their name. In our example, Leliana is found under Global > Followers.

In the Object Inspector, under the Apperance section, find the field for Head Morph>. For example, Leliana's Head Morph is hf_genfl_leliana.mor. This is what you need to rename your morph file.

Finding the Morph Name

Once you click the NPC's name, the Object Inspector will populate with the information of that character's file. Among the information is a section called Apperance. It is under this section you can find the information of the NPC's head morph's name. For Leliana, her head morph is listed as hf_genfl_leliana.mor.

Locate your exported .mor file and rename it the name listed (you can copy and paste it from the Toolset).

Rename your Morph

Your morph file must have the name that is listed as the NPC's head morph. Copy the name of the morph, then return to your file explorer and locate your file. Right click the file, click rename, and paste the name from the Toolset.

Now that the morph is in the correct format and has the correct name, now it can be used to override the default morph.

In your Documents Folder, click on BioWare > Dragon Age > Modules > Single Player > Overrride, and place the correctly named morph from the previous step. This will override the default morph.

Placing the File

Cut or copy the file you just renamed. Then, in your documents folder, click on Bioware > Dragon Age > Modules > Singe Player > Override. In this folder, paste the morph. In our example, we cut the file from its original place and placed in in this directory.

You can now play the game as usual and your new morph will be in it!

Launch the Game

The game can now be launched from Steam or Origin. If you have a save with your player near the NPC you edited, open that save and you can quickly check how the morph looks in game. If you desire to change it you can then return to the editing and export process. In our example, we opened a save with the Player Character at the party camp and initiated dialogue with Leliana, where you can see she has the new morph.

If you desire to change the morph after its in game, simply export a new morph, and follow the same renaming steps, and place it in the folder mentioned above.
